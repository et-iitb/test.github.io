Content accuracy and clarity Check if the content is accurate, follows correct symbols, standard norms of representation, uses appropriate terminology and is up-to-date with respect to relevant advances in the topic?

Look at: Audio visuals describing the topic, content, assessments, activities and examples.

Check whether:
  • The content uses scientifically accurate and grade-appropriate scientific information, phenomena, representations and pronunciations
  • The practice questions, activities and assessments (wherever present) are clear and correct.
  • The solutions of the practice questions, activities and assessments (wherever present) are clear and correct.
  • Advances in science are mentioned in the topic which are appropriate for the corresponding grade. For eg. Information about recent disease outbreaks like COVID etc.
Language comprehensibility Check if the language is appropriate and easy to understand for the students?

Look at: the voice over and text used in the video content, text content, assessments, activities and examples

Check whether:
  • The vocabulary used is familiar to the students of the particular age group.
  • An Indian accent is used and easy-to-follow for the students of the particular age group. There should not be use of slang or informal language like gonna, wanna, gotcha.
  • The sentences spoken or those appearing on-screen are short, legible and simple to follow.
  • Vernacular (Regional) language can be used to support comprehension.
Inclusivity Check if the product includes representation of the diverse groups that the students are likely to see around them?

Look at: audio-videos describing the topic, content, assessments, activities, examples.

Check whether:
  • The content includes diverse representations at least in terms of gender, ability (including children with special needs), appearance (such as skin and hair color, and body types), and setting (rural or urban environments).
  • There are no stereotypes associated with the identities presented to the students (e.g., individuals of a certain gender, race, or caste are not depicted in stereotypical professions or roles, such as mothers solely as homemakers or main characters always having a white complexion).
Curriculum alignment and Skill coverage Check if the product is aligned to the targeted curriculum and specific learning goals?

Look at: content of videos, assessments, activities, examples.

Check whether:
  • The product comprehensively covers all the topics outlined in the specific curriculum to which it is mapped (e.g., aligning with the NCERT curriculum if the product is mapped to it).
  • The content covers skills, pedagogy and assessment as prescribed by various bodies like NCF, NEP, NCERT
Bilingual use Note: Only evaluate if your content uses vernacular language. Check if the product includes use of English to introduce students to technical terms (like atoms, cell, organs, light rays etc., laws, names of processes like pasteurization, germination, etc.)?

Look at: content of videos, assessments, activities, examples.

Check whether:
  • The voice-over uses English scientific terms within a vernacular sentence while explaining the concept, such as "Tissue kya hota hai? Group of cell ko hum bolte hai tissue, matlab bahut sare cell jab apas mein combine ho jate hai wo ek tissue ka nirmaad karte hai.”
  • Onscreen, the English terms should be displayed, preferably with transliteration (i.e. English terms written in the vernacular script).
  • For assessment or activity questions, English technical terms should not be omitted from questions in the vernacular language. Vernacular questions should include English translations or incorporate English technical terms in brackets.
Interface Design: Enable Intuitive Use Check if the interface of the product follows principles of interaction design?

Look at: Visual elements such as buttons.

Check whether:
  • The student can easily find the visual elements and functions required to do the given task (e.g., pause, play, exit) and the buttons clearly indicate what function they do.
  • The interface contains sufficient and clear signs informing students about how to use elements of the interface to achieve a desired purpose/goal.
  • The interface is consistent i.e. it follows rules allowing similar operations and using similar elements for achieving similar tasks. There are no exceptions to a rule. (e.g., if the action to highlight any graphical object is by clicking the left mouse button and throughout the product it is the same on the interface)
  • The interface exploits intuitive mappings, meaning the controls to something closely resemble what they affect.
Navigation and pace Check if the interface provides students adequate control over navigation and pace within the product?

Look at: content, assessment, examples, activities

Check whether:
  • The interface contains obvious exit options.
  • The interface allows students to explore the content at their desired pace.
  • A single click will take the student to the desired video, assessment or activity
  • The students can easily move to a certain aspect in the content, e.g., position in the video.
Universal Design Check if the product is accessible to different types of students?

Look at: Audio, video content, assessment, examples, activities

Check whether:
The product at least has the following features:
  • Make it easier for students to see and hear content (for e.g., an option to increase font size)
  • Audio-only or video-only media provides a transcript and/ or on screen text/ caption.
  • Text-only media has a voice-over matching with the text.
  • Provide captions for all prerecorded / live audio (with or without video) content.
  • Possibility of recording an answer and hearing assessment questions as voice-overs.
Analytics for Students’ Progress Check if the interface provides an easy-to-interpret and actionable dashboard with student analytics?

Look at: Dashboard.

Check whether:
  • The dashboard should be visible and easy to find.
  • The dashboard clearly conveys the intended information about the students’ performance, progress, etc.
  • The dashboard should be easily ‘learnable’ (‘Learnable’ means how easy it is for a first-time user to do the basic tasks).
  • The dashboard should provide sufficient guidance to identify where the student needs additional support to make progress.
  • The dashboard analytics is customisable to the needs of the teacher.
  • The dashboard design should support processes including data processing, awareness, reflection and impact.
Content type-technology alignment Check if the visualization type chosen is suitable for teaching the corresponding content type?

Look at: Visualizations such as diagrams, videos, animations or simulations.

Check whether:
  • Diagram is present if content type is fact
  • Animation or video is available if content type is process
  • Simulation is provided if content type is concept/principle
Setting up a Game Environment (Note: Only evaluate Game based learning) Check if the game goals, rules, and game status is clearly communicated to students?

Look at Look at game rules and game goals.
Game Rule should tell How to play the game and
Game Goal should tell what will the student achieve after completing the game?

Check whether:
  • There are one / few rules of the game.
  • Rule(s) are simple to understand by the students.
  • The game rules are effectively communicated prior to playing so that the burden is not on the student to understand the rule.
  • Goal of the game is clear and is clearly communicated to the student.
Personalisation Check if feedback, motivation features and scaffolds varies with the student and it does not provide the same set of features for all the students?

Look at: the motivation features, scaffolds and feedback.

Check whether:
  • Personalised motivation: The motivational features change based on the student's response and performance. Example, the motivational message is different for a case where the student makes the first error from the time when the student has consistently provided wrong responses.
  • Personalised scaffolds (hints/ prompts/ support): The point to check is that the hints vary based on the attempt of the student and are not the same across all the options.
  • Personalised feedback: The students are given personalised feedback based on their past errors and/or performances.
Note: choose‘Completely incorporated’ if personalisation is seen in at least 2 out of 3 features. Mark as ‘Partially incorporated’ if 1 out of the 3 is seen and mark as not at all incorporated otherwise (0/ 3).
Adaptivity Check if the content adjusts to the student's profile, performance, interaction?
  • Profile: There is a diagnostic test at the beginning when the student engages with the content for the first time or at the beginning of every grade, or at the beginning of their learning journey. The product suggests content to the student based on the performance of the student in the test.
  • Performance: This includes adaptation based on students' response to the assessment questions.
  • Interaction: Adaptation is done based on student actions and behaviours such as time spent on each question, skipping content, requesting hints/help, random clicking pattern for answering and so on.
Note: Choose ‘completely incorporated’ if at least 2 out of 3 adaptation is seen. Mark as partially incorporated if 1 out of the 3 adaptation is seen and mark as not at all incorporated otherwise (0 out of 3). It take time to observe adaptivity. Adaptivity based on interaction might not be easily apparent at the front end. Please consider your backend design as well, if needed, and provide rationale and evidence accordingly.
Student autonomy/freedom Check if the student has the autonomy/freedom to choose what topic to choose among the suggested content?

Look at: how to choose the content, activity and assessment.

Check whether:
  • The product allows students to choose & unlock topics/subtopics that they want to do among the suggested topics.
  • This feature is seen consistently across all skills and topics.
Constructivist Approach Check if the product design / content promotes a hands-on and interactive approach to learning?

Look at: AV content, examples, activities

Check whether:
  • The product moves beyond mere transmission of information where the only role of the student is to be a passive listener.
  • The product allows the students to build their own meaning of the topic
  • The knowledge construction process takes into account the student's previous knowledge constructions, beliefs and attitudes
  • There are very well-sequenced set of multiple examples and applications of the same topic
  • There are in-video reflection spot questions that move beyond recall level questions. Such questions need to be solved within 2 minutes, and there is feedback provided for the question in the same video.
  • There is scope for the student to apply the concept using hands-on-activities (Note: this bullet point might not hold for IAV products without a platform)
Addressing learning gaps Check if the content effectively addresses common students' learning gaps?

Look at: AV content, examples, activities

Check whether:
  • The content provides opportunities to the student to identify their alternate conception/ learning gaps themselves that are related to the content being covered
  • This is followed by their effective redressal in the same video or content.
Example: A taller bottle need not always hold more liquid caters to identification of a possible learning gap, and explaining the rationale of how to understand it or how to arrive at this understanding refers to redressal
Content in context Check if the content has incorporated appropriate real-life context?

Look at: AV content, examples, activities and assessments.

Check whether:
  • The context provided is relevant to the context of the student wherever required.
  • The context provided is sufficient for the students to master the specific skill (e.g., showing weights might be relevant but not sufficient for the students to understand what to do with it or the concept of measurement)
  • The context does not contain scenarios involving violence, combat, gambling or stereotyping
  • The product provides opportunities to see the application of the skill in the lives of the students
Cognitive Engagement Check if the content presentation style and the graphics have the potential to engage students effectively in the learning process?

Look at: AV content

Check whether:
  • There is a use of conversational style rather than a formal speech (e.g., prefer the use of ‘us’ or ‘we’ over an instruction in passive voice)
  • Visual cues (e.g., highlight) to draw the student’s attention to a specific part of the screen.
  • Verbal cues (e.g., voice modulation) to engage the students.
  • Use of graphics that are relevant to the topic and can engage the student. There are no unnecessary graphics, sound, or presentation style that might engage the student but are not relevant to the content (e.g., background music or irrelevant texts in the background)
Motivational features Check if the product includes motivational factors that prompt students to interact further with the content?

Look at: AV content

Check whether:
  • Incentives like rewards (stars, badges etc), certificates depending on the students’ performance.
  • Visual progression of mastery levels or, labels like you are familiar, master, proficient in that topic
  • Motivational messages and/or sounds provided to students encouraging when student answered incorrectly or correctly
  • Motivation is seen not only when students are providing accurate responses but also when students are making (repeated) mistakes
  • Only if your product follows a gamified design: Performance-based unlocking of game activities/challenges in game/gamified design
Note: IAV products might not have external motivational features but motivation embedded in the video like words of encouragement, image, stars or sound
Cognitive Level Check if higher order thinking skills (HOTS) are being addressed in the content, assessments or activities?

Look at: AV content, assessments and activities.

Check whether:
  • Higher-order thinking skills (HOTS), relevant to the topic, are being sufficiently addressed in the content, examples or activities apart from lower-order thinking skills
  • The assessments or questions are sufficient to challenge the students in a meaningful way. That is, they include HOTs where the student can apply the targeted skills in a diverse context, or move beyond recall and understand level, in BLOOM’s taxonomy.
Quality of feedback Check if the assessment questions or activities provide students with useful and comprehensive feedback.

Look at: activities and assessment questions.

Check whether:
  • The feedback specifies whether the response was correct or incorrect.
  • The feedback provides the correct response and explanation if the answer was incorrect.
  • The feedback directs the students to revisit the content that was relevant to the question, in case of an incorrect response.
Learning Objective - Assessment Alignment Check if the assessment questions aligned to the specific learning objectives?

Look at: activities and assessment questions.

Check whether:
  • Assessment questions are present
  • Learning objective(s) is/are clearly specified without any ambiguity
  • All the assessment questions and the stated learning objectives are completely aligned
Appropriate Pedagogical Strategies Check if appropriate pedagogical strategies are used for teaching the content?

Look at: AV content, activities, examples and assessment.

Check whether:
  • The content uses play-based teaching rather than didactic communication. E.g., letter tracing to teach writing in literacy / dragging and dropping numbers in order to build number sense / use of local toys and games to build targeted skill(s) over text or video
  • There is a clear and explicit instruction to help the student master the specific skill. E.g., the targeted skill is comprehension (literacy) and the content explains how to master the skill in a play-based approach rather than just leave the users with a story and a set of questions and expect them to master the skill on their own
  • Use of child-centred pedagogy where the students gets many chances to express themselves which moves beyond typing long responses or choosing MCQ options
